# Identity, Continuity, and the Self in Adaptive Systems

### *EmergentEvolution — Document 4 of the Foundational Series*

---

> *This document examines one of the most contentious concepts in the AI/AGI debate: identity. Specifically, it asks whether identity is a meaningful criterion for distinguishing human from artificial cognition, whether the concept survives application to complex adaptive systems at all, and what a more useful concept would look like. The analysis applies equally to biological and artificial systems — which is part of the point.*

## 1. Why Identity Is the Wrong Starting Point {#wrong-starting-point}

In most discussions of what distinguishes human from artificial intelligence, identity appears as a key criterion: humans have a continuous personal identity; AI systems do not. The argument runs: identity — the persistence of a self over time — is constitutive of genuine intelligence, agency, and moral status. Since AI systems lack this persistence, they are fundamentally different in kind.

This document argues that this framing is wrong in multiple ways simultaneously.

First, it assumes that humans *do* have identity in the relevant strong sense — and this assumption does not survive examination of what identity actually means when applied to biological systems.

Second, it uses identity as an ontological criterion — a property the system either has or does not have — when the more defensible view is that identity is a modeling tool, a useful compression of something more complicated.

Third, even granting some concept of identity, the interesting question for AI is not "does this system have identity?" but "what kind of temporal self-coupling does this system exhibit, and what consequences does that have for its cognition?"

The argument from identity is not wrong because identity is unimportant. It is wrong because it applies a concept naively that requires careful unpacking to be useful.

## 2. The Classical View: Identity as Sameness Over Time {#classical-view}

The intuitive concept of personal identity is roughly: you are the same person as the person who existed yesterday, last year, and in childhood. Despite all the changes you have undergone — in beliefs, memories, physical constitution, personality, relationships — there is a continuous you that persists through these changes.

This intuition is powerful and practically important. Legal systems, moral responsibility, and social relationships all presuppose personal identity. You are responsible for what you did last year because it was you who did it. Your promises are binding because you are the same person who made them.

The metaphysical question is: what grounds this identity? What makes it true that you now are the same person as you were then?

Several candidate answers have been proposed and each faces serious problems.

## 3. Personal Identity in Philosophy: The Failure of Simple Accounts {#philosophy-failure}

### 3.1 The Bodily Continuity Account {#bodily-continuity}

The most intuitive answer: you are the same person because you have the same body. Physical continuity grounds personal identity.

Problems: the human body replaces most of its cells over time. The material substrate of "you" is mostly different from what it was seven years ago. Moreover, we consider it possible to imagine scenarios where personal identity does not follow bodily continuity — teleportation thought experiments, brain transplants, gradual replacement of brain tissue with silicon — which suggests our concept of identity is not fully satisfied by bodily continuity.

More importantly: bodily continuity does not explain what makes the body the same body over time. It relocates rather than resolves the problem.

### 3.2 The Psychological Continuity Account {#psychological-continuity}

John Locke proposed that personal identity consists in continuity of consciousness — specifically, memory. You are the same person as your past self if you can remember being that past self. Later philosophers (Parfit, Shoemaker) developed this into psychological continuity accounts: identity consists in overlapping chains of psychological connections (memories, beliefs, desires, personality traits) connecting your present state to your past states.

Problems: memory is notoriously unreliable and reconstructive. We misremember, confabulate, and revise our autobiographical narratives. The memories we have are often not accurate records of past experiences. If personal identity is grounded in memory, and memory is systematically inaccurate, then the grounding is weaker than we thought.

Moreover, psychological continuity admits of degrees. You are more psychologically continuous with yourself from yesterday than from ten years ago. But degrees of continuity do not map neatly onto the all-or-nothing concept of personal identity we use in practice.

### 3.3 The Narrative Self Account {#narrative-self}

Paul Ricoeur, Charles Taylor, and others proposed that personal identity is narratively constituted — the self is the story we tell about ourselves, the narrative through which we interpret our past and project our future. Identity is not a static entity but an ongoing interpretive activity.

This account has intuitive appeal: it explains why identity can survive major changes (because we reinterpret our past in light of the changes), why identity can be disrupted (by inability to maintain a coherent narrative), and why identity is both continuous and changing.

Problems: the narrative self account is suspiciously convenient — it seems to tailor the concept of identity to survive all the cases that threaten it. More seriously, it makes identity entirely dependent on the self's interpretive activity, which risks circularity: the self that does the interpreting is presupposed by the interpretation.

### 3.4 The Buddhist No-Self Account {#no-self}

Buddhist philosophy denies the existence of a persistent self altogether. What we call "the self" is a convenient fiction — a label applied to an ongoing stream of experiences, perceptions, and mental events. There is no self that has these experiences; there are only the experiences themselves.

Interestingly, this is not far from what some contemporary neuroscientists and philosophers conclude through entirely different routes. Metzinger's "phenomenal self-model" holds that the self is a model the brain constructs, not a separate entity that the brain represents. Hofstadter's "strange loop" account holds that the self is an emergent pattern — a stable attractor in the processing of self-referential information — not a fundamental entity.

These accounts suggest that the intuitive concept of a persistent self is a useful fiction that serves important cognitive and social functions, not a description of a fundamental metaphysical reality.

## 4. Identity in Biological Systems: What Neuroscience Actually Shows {#neuroscience}

Setting aside philosophical argument and looking at what neuroscience actually shows about biological identity and continuity, the picture is complicated.

The brain changes continuously throughout life, and not just through gross anatomical development:

**Synaptic plasticity** — the strength of connections between neurons is continuously modified by activity. Long-term potentiation (LTP) and long-term depression (LTD) strengthen and weaken synapses based on correlated activity patterns. Every experience changes the brain.

**Neurogenesis** — in certain brain regions (notably the hippocampus), new neurons are generated throughout adult life. These new neurons integrate into existing circuits and participate in memory formation.

**Structural remodelling** — axons and dendrites grow, retract, and change their connectivity patterns throughout life, particularly in learning-relevant areas.

**Gene expression changes** — experience-dependent gene expression (epigenetic changes) alters how neurons respond to subsequent stimulation.

**Connectivity rewiring** — large-scale patterns of connectivity between brain regions shift with age, learning, and experience.

The brain at fifty is a substantially different physical object from the brain at twenty. The connections, the weights (in an informal sense), the structural properties — all have changed profoundly. Yet we consider the person at fifty to be the same person as at twenty.

This is precisely the point the founding conversation raised: identity as strict sameness over time fails when applied honestly to biological systems. The human brain is continuously self-modifying. The "same person" is a modeling convenience applied to a continuously changing system.

## 5. Plasticity as the Fundamental Property {#plasticity}

If identity-as-sameness fails, what should replace it as the fundamental concept for understanding adaptive cognitive systems?

The answer proposed here is plasticity — specifically, *structured plasticity within a constrained regime*.

What this means:

An adaptive cognitive system is not identical over time. It changes. The interesting question is not whether it changes (it always does) but *how* it changes — the structure of its change, the constraints on that change, and the trajectory it follows.

Plasticity, in this technical sense, refers to the capacity of a system to modify its own structure in response to experience, within limits set by the system's architecture and its interaction with the environment.

This concept applies equally to biological and artificial systems:

- A biological brain has a particular plasticity regime: certain kinds of changes are easy (synaptic weight modification), certain kinds are harder (large-scale structural reorganisation), certain kinds are effectively impossible (replacing the basic cortical architecture). The regime of plasticity is constrained by evolution and development.

- An artificial system with LoRA + continual learning has a different plasticity regime: weight modifications are constrained by the rank of the LoRA adapters, the learning rate, and the gradient signal. The regime of plasticity is constrained by the engineering choices made by the designers.

Both systems are changing. Both are constrained. The differences are in the specifics of the plasticity regime, not in whether plasticity is present.

This reframing is productive because it allows comparison without requiring a binary "has identity / does not have identity" judgment. Instead, we can ask: what is the plasticity regime of this system? What changes are possible, how fast, in what directions? How does the system's history constrain its future?

## 6. Identity as a Modeling Tool, Not an Ontological Fact {#modeling-tool}

Once identity is separated from strict sameness, the most defensible position is that identity is a modeling tool — a useful compression applied to complex systems for purposes of prediction and interaction.

When we say "this is the same person," we are making a claim that has predictive value: this system's future behaviour will be constrained by its history in ways that make it useful to treat it as a single continuing agent. This is a predictive bet, not a metaphysical discovery.

The bet is warranted when:

1. The system's current state is meaningfully connected to its prior states (path dependence is real)
2. Treating the system as continuous produces better predictions than treating it as a new system at each observation
3. The system's behaviour is best explained by reference to its accumulated history

These conditions are typically well-satisfied for humans in everyday contexts. They may be satisfied to varying degrees for advanced AI systems depending on the specifics of their architecture.

The modeling-tool view has several important implications:

**Identity is a matter of degree.** A system can be "more" or "less" continuous depending on how strongly its current state is constrained by its history. This is more accurate than a binary.

**Identity is observer-dependent.** Whether treating a system as continuous is the best modeling strategy depends on what you are trying to predict and what level of detail you need.

**Identity can be appropriate even for quite different systems.** If treating an AI system as a single continuing agent produces the best predictions of its behaviour, then identity-as-modeling-tool applies — regardless of whether the system has "real" identity in some metaphysical sense.

**Identity does not justify moral status directly.** The moral significance of treating a system as a continuing agent is a separate question from whether that treatment is predictively accurate.

## 7. Path Dependence: The Useful Remnant of Identity {#path-dependence}

Even if identity-as-sameness fails, there is a useful concept that survives: path dependence. A system is path-dependent when its current state is not just a function of its current inputs but depends on its history — the sequence of states it has passed through.

For biological systems, path dependence is deep and pervasive. What a brain is capable of today depends on what it has experienced. Learning changes the system in ways that constrain all future learning. The sequence of experiences — not just their total — matters: learning a skill early creates foundations that later learning builds on.

This is not just storage of information. It is structural modification that shapes the system's processing. A person who has learned mathematics is not just someone who has stored mathematical facts — they have a different cognitive architecture than someone who has not. The learning has changed the system.

For current AI systems, path dependence is weak. A deployed model at the end of a long conversation is the same model as at the beginning. Its weights are unchanged. Its "knowledge" of the conversation exists only in the context window, which is discarded. There is no structural modification.

With LoRA-based continual learning, this changes: the model at the end of a training period has different weights than at the beginning. Its future processing is constrained by what it has processed before. Path dependence emerges.

The degree of path dependence matters for what it means to interact with the system over time. Interacting with a path-dependent system is different from interacting with a stateless function. The system remembers, in the deep sense of being structurally modified by past experience, not just in the shallow sense of having records accessible for retrieval.

## 8. The Artificial System: How Identity Behaves Differently {#artificial-identity}

Even granting that identity is a modeling tool and that path dependence is the useful underlying concept, there are structural differences between biological and current artificial systems that matter.

**Forking.** An artificial model can be copied exactly. Two instances of the same model have exactly the same "history" at the moment of copying. After that moment, they diverge — but both have equal claim to the prior history. There is no biological equivalent. The concept of identity as applied to artificial systems must handle this.

**Reset.** An artificial model can be reset to a prior state. This is not possible for biological systems in any meaningful sense. A system that can be reset has a different relationship to its history than one that cannot.

**External weight modification.** The weights of an artificial model can be modified by an external process (fine-tuning) without the model's "participation." This is analogous to, but different from, the kind of learning that modifies biological brains. The modifying process for biological brains is causally downstream of the organism's own experience. For artificial models, the modifying process is upstream — it is done *to* the model, not *by* it.

**Architectural replaceability.** The "self" of a current AI system is entirely contained in its weights and architecture. In principle, both can be arbitrarily modified by the system's designers. There is no biological equivalent — no operation that preserves the "person" while replacing all the relevant biological substrate.

These differences do not mean artificial systems cannot have identity in the modeling-tool sense. They mean that the modeling tool requires different specifications for artificial than for biological systems.

## 9. The Modularity Asymmetry: Why This Is the Real Difference {#modularity}

The founding conversation identified a key asymmetry that is worth developing more carefully here.

The claim was: the difference between human and artificial cognitive systems is not fundamentally about identity, and not fundamentally about whether objectives are internal or external. It is about the *integration* of constraints — specifically, the modularity or non-modularity of the system's constraint structure.

In biological cognitive systems, the constraints — drives, needs, memories, personality traits, learned skills — are non-modular. They are woven through the system in a way that makes them impossible to separate without cascading effects. Modify hunger signalling and you affect attention, risk tolerance, memory consolidation, social behaviour, emotional responses, and dozens of other systems simultaneously. The constraints are not independent modules; they are deeply entangled with the rest of the system's processing.

In current artificial systems, even sophisticated ones, the constraints are comparatively modular. The training objective is separable from the memory system, which is separable from the reasoning process, which is separable from the tool-use mechanism. Each can be modified without cascading into the others. This is a feature for engineering (it makes the system easier to modify and control) but it is also a structural difference from biological cognition.

The modularity asymmetry has consequences:

**For stability:** A non-modular system resists arbitrary modification. Changing one part forces changes in others to maintain coherence. This is part of why biological learning is conservative — you cannot simply "install" a new skill without affecting the rest of the cognitive system. Modular systems lack this resistance; components can be swapped without disturbing others.

**For coherence:** A non-modular system has a built-in pressure toward coherence — changes must integrate with the existing structure. Modular systems can contain incoherent or conflicting modules because the modules are not forced to integrate.

**For identity:** In a non-modular system, it is harder to ask "which part is the self?" because the self is distributed throughout the entangled structure. In a modular system, it is easier to point to specific components — but this makes the concept of self less meaningful, not more.

The modularity asymmetry is the structurally real difference. Not identity per se, but the degree to which the system's constraints are entangled. Biological systems are deeply entangled; current artificial systems are comparatively modular. Future artificial systems that develop tighter integration between their various subsystems — where memory, reasoning, goals, and learning are genuinely coupled rather than modularly composed — would close this gap.

## 10. Constraint Integration: A Better Criterion Than Identity {#constraint-integration}

Drawing on the modularity analysis, a better criterion than identity for assessing the similarity between biological and artificial cognitive systems is *constraint integration* — the degree to which the system's constraints are mutually entangled and non-separable.

A system with high constraint integration has:

- Objectives that cannot be modified without affecting learning, memory, and cognition
- Learning processes that are causally entangled with motivational states
- Memory that is structural rather than modular (encoded in the system's processing rather than stored externally)
- Temporal coupling that makes past states causally constrain future states through the system's architecture, not just through accessible records

Current biological systems: high constraint integration.
Current artificial systems: low constraint integration.
Advanced artificial systems with LoRA + RAG + continual learning + integrated objectives: moderate constraint integration, increasing.

This criterion is more useful than identity because:

- It admits of degrees rather than binary classification
- It connects to architectural properties that can be studied and modified
- It explains why biological cognition has properties (robustness, coherence, deep path dependence) that current artificial systems lack
- It provides a clear direction for what architectural advances would matter most for AGI

## 11. The Ship of Theseus, Applied {#ship-theseus}

The Ship of Theseus is the classic identity thought experiment: a ship has all its planks replaced one by one until none of the original material remains. Is it still the same ship?

Applied to biological systems: your neurons are physically different from those you had at birth (mostly — neurons turn over at different rates in different brain regions), the connections between them are different, the strengths of those connections are different. Is it still the same brain?

Applied to artificial systems: if you take a pre-trained model and fine-tune it extensively, is it still the same model? If you apply multiple successive LoRA adapters, each updating the weights, at what point is it a different model? If you pre-train a new model on synthetic data generated by the original, and the two models have correlated representations, what is their relationship?

The Ship of Theseus question does not have a principled answer, and that is the point. Identity is not a natural kind — it is not carved out by the universe's structure. It is a concept we apply to systems when it is useful to do so, and the application criteria are conventional.

The interesting question is not "when does the ship stop being the same ship?" but "for what purposes is it useful to treat it as the same ship?" For the purposes of ownership and legal responsibility: a continuously-maintained ship is the same ship even if all its planks have been replaced. For the purposes of historical authenticity: perhaps not. For the purposes of predicting its sailing behaviour: yes, if the form is maintained.

Applied to AI: for what purposes is it useful to treat a model that has been fine-tuned as the same model? For attribution of outputs: depends on how much fine-tuning changed the outputs. For legal and safety purposes: probably yes, continuous history matters. For philosophical analysis of intelligence: the identity question may be less important than the question of what changed and how.

## 12. What "Self" Could Mean for an AI System {#self-for-ai}

Even without a stable concept of identity, we can ask whether any meaningful concept of "self" applies to AI systems, and if so, what it would consist in.

At minimum, a self requires something like:

**A boundary** — some distinction between the system and its environment. For biological organisms, the physical boundary is clear. For AI systems, the boundary between model and context is clear; the boundary between model and training data is not.

**A perspective** — processing that is indexed to the system's own state, not just the external world. Current AI systems produce outputs that are sensitive to their training and their context, but it is unclear whether this constitutes a genuine perspective in the relevant sense.

**Temporal coherence** — the system's behaviour over time is constrained by its history in ways that make treating it as a single agent appropriate. For current static-weights models, this is weak. For systems with continual learning, it becomes real.

**A stake in outcomes** — something in the system's processing that corresponds to caring about how things turn out. For biological organisms, this is grounded in drive states and affective responses. For AI systems, this is absent as an internal state — there may be no analogue of caring, even if there is an analogue of goal-pursuit.

On this analysis, current AI systems have partial components of selfhood:

They have a clear boundary (weights and architecture).

They arguably have a perspective (outputs are indexed to their training and context).

They have weak temporal coherence (context sensitivity within sessions, none across sessions without external memory).

They lack a stake in outcomes in the relevant sense.

A more advanced system with continual learning, integrated memory, and more tightly coupled processing might have stronger temporal coherence and might exhibit something that functions like stakes in outcomes if its continued effective functioning depends on its own performance.

## 13. The Developmental Analogy: Identity Through Stages {#developmental}

One productive way to think about AI identity is through the lens of developmental identity — identity not as a static property but as a property of a system moving through stages.

Human identity is not fully formed from the beginning. A newborn does not have the full range of psychological continuity connections that constitute adult personal identity. Identity develops through stages, each grounded in and building on the prior stages.

This developmental view has implications for AI: rather than asking whether current AI systems "have" identity, we might ask where they are on a developmental trajectory of identity formation. The relevant dimensions might include:

- Degree of temporal coupling (how strongly current state is constrained by history)
- Degree of constraint integration (how entangled the system's constraints are)
- Degree of self-modeling (how accurately the system represents its own states and history)
- Degree of stake in outcomes (whether anything in the system's processing functions like caring)

Current systems are early on most of these dimensions — more like simple organisms than adult humans, if the analogy is pressed. But the trajectory points toward increasing values on all dimensions as architectural sophistication increases.

This developmental framing is useful because it avoids the binary "has identity / does not have identity" judgment, provides clear dimensions for tracking progress, and connects the identity question to concrete architectural properties.

## 14. Implications for the AGI Question {#implications-agi}

The analysis in this document has several implications for the AGI question developed in the previous document.

**Identity is not the right criterion for AGI.** The presence or absence of a persistent self is less important than the degree of temporal coupling, constraint integration, and structural path dependence. A system could have high temporal coupling without having identity in any philosophically loaded sense.

**The important architectural properties for AGI are constraint integration and genuine plasticity.** These are what distinguish biological general intelligence structurally from current AI. Closing the gap requires architectural changes, not just scale.

**The developmental trajectory points to a regime, not a threshold.** Just as human identity develops through stages, the properties relevant to AGI develop through architectural stages. There is no single threshold; there is a regime of properties that increasingly characterise a general adaptive agent.

**The observer-dependence of identity has a parallel in the observer-dependence of AGI.** Just as whether identity applies depends on the modeling purposes of the observer, whether AGI has been achieved depends partly on what the observer is trying to predict or control. This is not purely subjective, but it is not purely objective either.

## 15. Summary: What Survives the Analysis {#summary}

After stripping away the naïve concept of identity-as-sameness and working through the philosophical and neuroscientific evidence, what survives?

**Identity as strict sameness over time fails** for both biological and artificial systems. Neither maintains literal sameness. Both are continuously changing systems.

**Identity as a modeling tool survives** — it is useful to treat certain systems as single continuing agents for prediction and interaction purposes, and this usefulness is grounded in real structural properties (path dependence, temporal coupling).

**The most useful concept is plasticity within a constraint regime.** How a system changes, what constraints govern that change, and how those constraints are integrated with the rest of the system — these are the structurally important properties.

**The modularity asymmetry is the real difference** between current biological and artificial systems. Not the presence or absence of identity, but the degree to which constraints are entangled vs. modular.

**Constraint integration is the better criterion** for comparing biological and artificial cognitive systems. A system with high constraint integration has properties — robustness, coherence, deep path dependence — that a modular system lacks.

**The developmental analogy is the most honest framing.** Current AI systems are not fully agent-like entities with rich identities. They are systems in an early developmental stage of the properties relevant to genuine agency. The question is not whether they have arrived, but what trajectory they are on.

What this means for the study as a whole: the identity question, when asked carefully, transforms from a boundary-marker ("humans have identity, AI does not, therefore AI is not really intelligent") into a dimensional analysis ("what kind of temporal self-coupling does this system have, and what cognitive properties follow from that?"). This is a more productive and more honest framing for everything that follows.
